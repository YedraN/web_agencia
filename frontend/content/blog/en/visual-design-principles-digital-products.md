Visual design is often dismissed as "making things look good." In reality, it's a communication discipline. Every color choice, spacing decision, and typographic pairing either clarifies or obscures the product's purpose. Good visual design makes the complex feel simple.

## Hierarchy: the invisible guide

Visual hierarchy is the arrangement of elements in order of importance. The human eye scans pages in predictable patterns — typically F-shaped or Z-shaped depending on the layout. Use size, weight, color, and position to create clear focal points.

The primary action on any page should be visually dominant. Secondary actions should recede. If two buttons compete for attention, the user hesitates. Hierarchy eliminates that hesitation.

## Color with intention

Color does three jobs simultaneously: it communicates brand identity, conveys meaning (red for errors, green for success), and creates contrast for readability. A well-crafted color palette has one or two dominant hues, a neutral background, and one accent color reserved exclusively for interactive elements.

Avoid using color alone to convey information. Users with color vision deficiency (about 8% of men) will miss those cues. Always pair color with icons, labels, or patterns.

## Typography that breathes

Type is the backbone of digital interfaces. Choose a primary typeface for headings (display personality) and a secondary for body text (prioritize readability). Keep line length between 50 and 75 characters. Use adequate line height — 1.5x for body text is a good baseline.

The biggest typography mistake? Sizes that don't create enough contrast between levels. Your H1 and body text should differ by at least 10–12 points. If everything looks similar, nothing is important.

## Space as a design element

Whitespace isn't wasted space — it's breathing room for the eye. Generous spacing between sections, around buttons, and inside cards improves readability and perceived sophistication. Studies show that increased whitespace can improve comprehension by up to 20%.

## Consistency builds trust

A consistent visual system — same button styles, same icon set, same spacing scale — signals professionalism. Users trust products that look coherent. A design system or component library enforces this consistency across every screen.
